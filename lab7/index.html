Lab #7

OSPF in a multi-area network

The purpose of this lab is to show how routing in complex topologies can be managed by decomposing them in areas.
First of all, we solve the network addressing problem, by subnetting a block of 128 IPv4 addresses in a suitable number of subnets.
Then, we create the network topology in GNS3 and configure the routers.
Finally, we analyze the routes established by OSPF.

Subnetting

The network topology we want to create in GNS3 is depicted in the following picture.


The network consists of 7 routers and 5 end systems. Routers are connected by means of 9 point-to-point links.
For the sake of OSPF routing, we partition the routers in three areas.
Area 0 consists of routers R4, R5 and R6.
Area 1 consists of routers R0 and R1.
Area 2 consists of routers R2 and R3.
As the picture above shows, network addressing requires 14 subnets.
We assume that a block of 128 contiguous IPv4 addresses is available: 192.168.24.0/25.
We perform subnetting with variable length mask with:
  • netmask /30 for 9 point to point links between routers;
  • netmask /28 for 5 links from routers to endpoints.
The following table shows how the available address range can be split into the required 14 subnets.

Notice that, after subnetting, a few small address ranges remain unused.

Preparation steps

Before this experiment is executed, it is required that you have previously configured GNS3 to make it able to instantiate a Docker container of a Linux-based router based on the FRR (Free Range Routing) software suite, as described in Lab #2.

Traffic generation and results analysis

  • In PC1 terminal execute the following commands: 
    ping 192.168.24.82 -c 2
    and verify that answers are received from PC2.
    ping 192.168.24.98 -c 2
    and verify that answers are received from PC3.
    ping 192.168.24.114 -c 2
    and verify that answers are received from PC4.
    ping 192.168.24.50 -c 2
    and verify that answers are received from PC5.



  • In PC1 terminal execute the following commands: 
    trace 192.168.24.82 -P 1
    and verify that packets to PC2 are routed through R0 and R1 (shortest path).
    trace 192.168.24.98 -P 1
    and verify that packets to PC3 are routed through R0, R4, R5 and R2 (shortest path).
    trace 192.168.24.114 -P 1
    and verify that packets to PC4 are routed through R0, R4, R5 and R3 (shortest path).
    trace 192.168.24.50 -P 1
    and verify that packets to PC5 are routed through R0, R4 and R6 (shortest path).



  • Right-click on the link connecting R0 and R1 and suspend the link.
    Wait about 30 seconds until OSPF converges to an alternative route.
  • In PC1 terminal execute the following command: 
    trace 192.168.24.82 -P 1
    and verify that packets are now routed through R0, R4 and R1.



  • Right-click on the link connecting R4 and R5 and suspend the link.
    Wait about 30 seconds until OSPF converges to an alternative route.
  • In PC1 terminal execute the following command: 
    trace 192.168.24.98 -P 1
    and verify that packets are now routed through R0, R4, R6, R5 and R2.



  • Right-click on the link connecting R3 and R5 and suspend the link.
    Wait about 30 seconds until OSPF converges to an alternative route.
  • In PC1 terminal execute the following command: 
    trace 192.168.24.114 -P 1
    and verify that packets are now routed through R0, R4, R6, R5, R2 and R3.



  • Right-click on the suspended links and resume them.
    Wait about 30 seconds until OSPF converges again.
  • In R0 auxiliary console execute the following commands: 
    vtysh
